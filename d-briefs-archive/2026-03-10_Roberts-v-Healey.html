Case Brief

Roberts v. Healey — Legal Malpractice / Negligence

Case Identification
Karin M. Roberts, et al. v. James Sean Healey and James Sean Healey, P.C.
991 S.W.2d 873 (Tex. App. 1999)
Court of Appeals of Texas, Houston (14th Dist.)
February 11, 1999
Justice Lee (Panel: Justices Lee, Anderson, and O'Neill)
No. 14-96-01306-CV
Facts
Karin Roberts married Daniel Charles Kennedy in 1991; they had two children, Ashli (born 1991) and Alexis (born 1994). During the marriage, Kennedy developed a drug habit and became increasingly unstable and violent. By September 1994, Kennedy's behavior was so erratic that Karin asked him to move out. On October 11, 1994, Karin contacted attorney James Sean Healey's office to discuss divorce representation and scheduled an appointment for October 13. Kennedy made harassing telephone calls and pages to Karin, including several death threats which she relayed to Healey. Karin also told Healey about Kennedy's drug use. At Healey's request, Karin prepared a narrative outlining Kennedy's violent history to be used in obtaining a temporary restraining order. Healey converted this into an affidavit, which Karin signed. Healey filed an application for a restraining order with the original divorce petition, BUT NEVER MADE ANY EFFORT TO OBTAIN A SIGNED PROTECTIVE ORDER despite repeated calls from Karin and her mother, Marjorie Roberts. Because of Kennedy's behavior, Karin moved to a new apartment with her daughters. On one occasion, she called police asking them to keep Kennedy from following her because she was afraid he would locate her new apartment. On November 1, 1994, Kennedy attempted suicide, which Karin reported to Healey. Kennedy was admitted to a psychiatric ward but checked out two days later. He left a note on Karin's apartment door; fearful he had learned where she lived, Karin immediately took the note to Healey's office. On November 16, 1994, Kennedy (high on cocaine) confronted Karin in her apartment parking lot as she was leaving for work. He forced her to accompany him to the apartment, broke in, and SHOT AND KILLED his two children. He also shot and wounded his mother-in-law, Marjorie, then committed suicide.
Procedural History
Appellants (Karin and Marjorie Roberts) sued Healey for negligence, gross negligence, breach of contract, breach of warranty, and Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA) violations. The trial court granted Healey's motion for SUMMARY JUDGMENT on all causes of action. Appellants appealed, bringing a single point of error asserting the trial court erred in granting summary judgment. The Court of Appeals AFFIRMED in part and REVERSED AND REMANDED in part.
Issue(s)
1. Did the trial court err in granting summary judgment on the negligence claim based on Healey's argument that his failure to obtain a protective order was not the proximate/producing cause of the deaths and injuries as a matter of law? 2. Can an attorney be held liable for negligence when the criminal acts of a third party (the estranged husband) intervene between the attorney's alleged negligence and the plaintiff's injuries? 3. Did the trial court err in granting summary judgment on the DTPA and breach of contract/warranty claims?
Holding
The Court of Appeals AFFIRMED the summary judgment on the DTPA and breach of contract/warranty claims, but REVERSED the summary judgment on the NEGLIGENCE claim and REMANDED for further proceedings. The court held that Healey had NOT disproved causation as a matter of law, and that fact questions existed regarding whether Healey's failure to obtain a protective order was the proximate cause of the deaths and injuries.
Rule of Law
1. PROXIMATE CAUSE: In attorney malpractice cases involving criminal acts of third parties, the criminal act does not necessarily break the chain of causation as a matter of law. Whether the criminal act was foreseeable is generally a question of fact for the jury. 2. SUMMARY JUDGMENT STANDARD: In reviewing summary judgment, courts indulge every reasonable inference in the non-movant's favor and take evidence favorable to the non-movant as true. Summary judgment is proper only if there is no genuine issue of material fact. 3. LEGAL MALPRACTICE: An attorney's failure to perform a duty (here, obtaining a protective order) can be the proximate cause of injuries when the harm was a foreseeable consequence of that failure. 4. PROTECTIVE ORDERS: Under Texas Family Code § 71.11 (1994), protective orders may prohibit threatening conduct, harassment, going near residences, and other conduct reasonably likely to harass, alarm, or endanger family members.
Reasoning / Rationale
CAUSATION ANALYSIS: The court rejected Healey's argument that Kennedy's criminal acts broke the chain of causation as a matter of law. The court cited precedent establishing that "intervening criminal acts of third persons, foreseeable or not, do not operate as a superseding cause which relieves a negligent actor of liability." Whether the criminal act was foreseeable is a fact question for the jury, not a legal question for summary judgment. EVIDENCE OF FORESEEABILITY: The court found ample evidence suggesting Kennedy's violence was foreseeable: - Karin reported Kennedy's death threats to Healey - Healey knew about Kennedy's drug use - Kennedy had a documented history of violence (hitting Karin, threatening with gun, destroying property) - Kennedy had made harassing calls and left violent messages - Karin had moved to a new apartment specifically because she feared Kennedy would locate her - Kennedy had attempted suicide shortly before the killings - Karin repeatedly contacted Healey's office about the urgent need for protection BREACH OF DUTY: Healey never made any effort to obtain a signed protective order despite: - Having an affidavit from Karin detailing Kennedy's violent history - Karin's repeated calls expressing fear - The mother's repeated calls - The imminent danger being apparent The court concluded that genuine fact issues existed regarding whether Healey's negligence caused the deaths and injuries, making summary judgment inappropriate on the negligence claim. DTPA/BREACH OF CONTRACT CLAIMS AFFIRMED: The court affirmed summary judgment on these claims because appellants failed to produce evidence of Healey's knowledge that Karin's parents were paying for the representation (required for the DTPA claim) and the contract claims lacked adequate proof of damages or breach.

Analysis & Significance
This is a significant Texas legal malpractice case establishing that attorneys can be held liable for negligence when they fail to take protective measures in domestic violence situations, and that the criminal acts of the abuser do not automatically break the chain of causation. KEY SIGNIFICANCE: - ATTORNEY DUTY: Clarifies that family law attorneys have a duty to pursue available legal protections (like protective orders) when client safety is at risk - CAUSATION IN THIRD-PARTY CRIME CASES: Reaffirms that criminal acts don't necessarily sever causation; foreseeability is a jury question - SUMMARY JUDGMENT LIMITS: Demonstrates that when fact questions exist about foreseeability and causation, summary judgment is inappropriate TRAGIC CONTEXT: This case involves horrific facts—the murders of two young children (ages 2 and 3) and the shooting of their grandmother—highlighting the life-or-death stakes in family law practice when domestic violence is present. The court's decision allowed the case to proceed to trial, potentially providing some avenue of justice for the victims. PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS: The case serves as a warning to family law practitioners about the importance of: - Promptly seeking protective orders when domestic violence is alleged - Documenting client communications about safety concerns - Treating threats seriously, even when clients seem to be managing the situation - Understanding that failure to act can have fatal consequences SUBSEQUENT TREATMENT: This case is frequently cited in Texas legal malpractice jurisprudence regarding causation and attorney duties in domestic violence contexts.
