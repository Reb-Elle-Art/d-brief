# Case Brief: Wilcox v. Trautz
**Citation:** 427 Mass. 326 (1998)  
**Court:** Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts  
**Date Argued:** March 5, 1998  
**Date Decided:** April 21, 1998  
**Judge:** Greaney, J. (Wilkins, C.J., Abrams, Lynch, Fried, Marshall, & Ireland, JJ. concurring)

---

## PROCEDURAL HISTORY

Plaintiff Carol Wilcox and defendant John Trautz lived together as an unmarried couple for approximately 25 years. After their separation, Wilcox brought an action in Probate and Family Court seeking:
1. Declaration that a written cohabitation agreement was invalid
2. Injunction preventing Trautz from transferring the home
3. Constructive trust or damages (quantum meruit)

The Probate and Family Court judge conducted a bifurcated trial:
- **Phase 1:** Found the written agreement invalid and unenforceable
- **Phase 2:** Denied constructive trust and implied contract claims; awarded ~$30,000 in quantum meruit damages

Defendant appealed. The Supreme Judicial Court transferred the case on its own motion and **REVERSED**, holding the agreement valid and enforceable.

---

## FACTS

**Relationship (1967-1992):**
- Parties began living together in 1967 (plaintiff age 22, defendant age 25)
- Both employed at same supermarket (defendant earned ~$100/week more)
- Moved through three residences, all titled solely in defendant's name

**Financial Arrangement:**
- Plaintiff contributed $25/week toward household expenses (1973-1992)
- Plaintiff paid for all food, clothing, cooking, cleaning, laundry
- Purchased flooring, carpeting, patio furniture, deck
- Shared costs of TV, oven, refrigerator, air conditioner
- **Result:** Plaintiff had no savings, small pension; defendant acquired real estate and airplane

**The Agreement (March 1989):**
- Drafted by defendant's attorney after plaintiff became involved with another person
- Defendant presented agreement with ultimatum: sign or relationship ends
- Defendant advised plaintiff to seek legal counsel; she did not
- Agreement signed before notary public

**Key Agreement Terms:**
- "What's mine is mine and what's yours is yours"
- Each party's earnings and property remain separate
- Services rendered are voluntary, without expectation of compensation
- Contributions to mortgage deemed rent only, creating no property interest
- Money transfers deemed loans

**Trigger:** In 1992, defendant gave 30 days' notice to vacate; plaintiff refused and occupied separate bedroom until trial.

---

## ISSUE

Whether a written agreement between unmarried cohabitants defining their property rights is enforceable under Massachusetts law.

**Sub-issues:**
1. Does public policy prohibit enforcement of cohabitation agreements?
2. Is an agreement enforceable if entered to secure sexual fidelity?
3. Are antenuptial agreement standards (fairness and reasonableness) applicable?

---

## HOLDING

**Yes.** Unmarried cohabitants may lawfully contract concerning property, financial, and other matters relevant to their relationship. Such agreements are **valid and enforceable** according to ordinary contract law principles, except to the extent that sexual services constitute the only or dominant consideration.

The court **VACATED** the judgment and ordered entry of a new judgment declaring the agreement valid and enforceable.

---

## REASONING

### 1. Historical Context & Evolution of Law

**Old Rule (pre-1998):**
- Agreements in consideration of cohabitation were unenforceable (Zytka v. Dmochowski, 302 Mass. 63 (1938))
- Early cases precluded enforcement if parties expected to cohabit

**Intermediate Position:**
- Margolies v. Hopkins, 401 Mass. 88 (1987): Oral promises valid if "illicit sexual relations were not an inherent aspect of the agreement or a serious and not merely an incidental part"

**Social Changes:**
- "Social mores regarding cohabitation between unmarried parties have changed dramatically"
- Cohabitation now "relatively common and accepted"
- Many cohabiting relationships resemble marriages in duration, commingling of funds, joint purchases, children
- Massachusetts does not recognize common law marriage

### 2. Adoption of New Rule

The court **expressly adopted** the view that:
> "unmarried cohabitants may lawfully contract concerning property, financial, and other matters relevant to their relationship"

**Rationale:**
- Encourages written agreements ("considerable benefits to be gained")
- Provides certainty on termination or death
- No reason to uphold property arrangements but withhold enforcement of separation agreements
- Other jurisdictions uniformly recognize such agreements (citing cases from CA, NJ, AZ, CT, NE, NV, NM, NY, PA, OR, TX, WY)

**Limitations:**
- Agreement unenforceable if "sexual services constitute the only, or dominant, consideration"
- Agreement unenforceable if enforcement contrary to other public policy grounds
- Does NOT create marital property rights (court explicitly preserved distinction between married and unmarried couples)

### 3. Application to This Agreement

**Valid Purpose:**
- Principal purpose was clarifying rights to assets acquired during relationship
- Securing sexual fidelity was NOT the sole or dominant purpose
- Agreement explicitly stated sexual services were not consideration
- 1992 infidelity merely triggered performance; didn't define the agreement

**No Antenuptial Standards Applied:**
- Unmarried agreements NOT governed by antenuptial agreement requirements
- No requirement of "fairness and reasonableness" review
- Governed by ordinary contract law principles

**Contract Validity Elements Met:**
- Both adults with capacity to contract
- Full disclosure of financial status
- Plaintiff advised to seek counsel (chose not to)
- No fraud, overreaching, or unconscionability
- No claim of inability to support herself
- No coercion or duress (found by trial judge)

### 4. Preserved Distinctions

Court emphasized it did NOT:
- Recognize common law marriage
- Extend marital property rights to unmarried couples
- Permit equitable division of property between unmarried parties
- Retreat from importance of marriage institution

Cited prior cases preserving distinctions:
- Collins v. Guggenheim: No equitable distribution for cohabitants
- Feliciano v. Rosemar Silver Co.: No loss of consortium rights
- Davis v. Misiano: No separate support/alimony

---

## RULE

**Massachusetts Rule on Cohabitation Agreements:**

Unmarried cohabitants may enter into enforceable written agreements concerning property, financial, and other matters. Such agreements are valid if:

1. They comply with ordinary contract law principles
2. Sexual services are not the **only or dominant consideration**
3. Enforcement would not violate other public policy grounds

These agreements are **NOT** subject to antenuptial agreement standards (fairness/reasonableness review). Courts will not inquire into fairness beyond general contract principles.

---

## CONCURRENCE/DISSENT

**None.** All seven justices joined in the opinion authored by Justice Greaney.

---

## SIGNIFICANCE

1. **Landmark Case:** First Massachusetts case expressly validating written cohabitation agreements
2. **Policy Shift:** Overruled prior cases suggesting all cohabitation agreements were unenforceable
3. **Practical Impact:** Encourages unmarried couples to formalize financial arrangements
4. **Clear Boundaries:** Explicitly preserves distinction between marital and non-marital property rights
5. **National Alignment:** Brought Massachusetts in line with majority of jurisdictions recognizing such agreements

---

## KEY QUOTES

> "Social mores regarding cohabitation between unmarried parties have changed dramatically in recent years and living arrangements that were once criticized are now relatively common and accepted."

> "We do well to recognize the benefits to be gained by encouraging unmarried cohabitants to enter into written agreements respecting these matters, as the consequences for each partner may be considerable on termination of the relationship or, in particular, in the event of the death of one of the partners."

> "[W]hile cohabitation without marriage does not give rise to the property and financial rights which normally attend the marital relation, neither does cohabitation disable the parties from making an agreement within the normal rules of contract law."

> "An agreement between two unmarried parties is not governed by the threshold requirements that apply to an antenuptial agreement... a court is no more entitled to inquire into its fairness and reasonableness than it is in respect to contracts generally."

---

## NOTES

- **Footnote 4:** If parties later marry, cohabitation agreement becomes invalid; antenuptial/postnuptial rules then apply
- **Footnote 7:** Court expressly declined to adopt Marvin v. Marvin (CA) approach granting property rights without express contract
- **No children involved:** Court noted different analysis might apply to agreements affecting children's rights
