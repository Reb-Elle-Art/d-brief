Relevant Facts

Focus on facts material to the court's decision

  • Victim: Angel Figueroa (murdered)
  • Defendant: John M. Hine, Jr. — suspected of involvement in the killing
  • April 2, 1982: Defendant went to State Police CPAC office in Worcester after police left message with his wife
  • April 2 Interrogation: Four officers present initially; Trooper McDonald read Miranda warnings from printed card; defendant made oral waiver
  • The Forgery: Officers claimed defendant signed Miranda waiver card, but judge found signature was forged — "an attempt to copy [the defendant's] signature"
  • April 2 Statements: Defendant denied murder but made inculpatory statements (admitted inducing victim from his home and delivering him to Eddy Rivera for heroin)
  • April 5, 1982: Defendant questioned again while in custody on parole violation warrant; accompanied by attorney Walter A. Rojcewicz
  • April 5 Issue: Defendant was not given Miranda warnings; allegedly verified his April 2 statements
  • Handwriting Evidence: Defense expert Catherine A. Cusack testified signature was forged; Commonwealth expert John Swanson's testimony "largely supportive" of this conclusion
Procedural Posture

Trace the case's path through the judicial system

  • June 8, 1983: Indictment returned in Superior Court for murder of Angel Figueroa
  • Motion to Suppress: Defendant filed motion to suppress statements from April 2 and 5, 1982
  • Motion Hearing: Heard by Judge Robert V. Mulkern in Superior Court
  • Judge's Rulings:
    • Granted suppression of April 2 Miranda card (forged signature)
    • Granted suppression of April 2 admissions (despite finding no prejudice)
    • Granted suppression of April 5 admissions (no Miranda warnings given)
    • Denied motion to dismiss indictment for prosecutorial misconduct
  • Interlocutory Appeals: Both Commonwealth and defendant filed cross-applications for interlocutory appeal under Mass. R. Crim. P. 15(b)(2)
  • Single Justice: Justice Lynch granted both applications and referred to full bench
  • SJC Review: Full Supreme Judicial Court review
Legal Question(s) Presented

Write in question format. Include all sub-issues if applicable.

  1. Did the motion judge err in finding that the defendant's signature on the April 2 Miranda card was forged?
  2. If the signature was forged, must the defendant's April 2 statements be suppressed if he received and validly waived his Miranda rights orally?
  3. Did the judge err in finding that the defendant was not advised of and did not waive his Miranda rights prior to the April 5 custodial interrogation?
  4. Should the indictment be dismissed due to police misconduct in forging the Miranda card?
Court's Decision

REVERSED in part, AFFIRMED in part:

  • ✓ April 2 Statements: Reversed suppression — statements are admissible (no error)
  • ✓ April 2 Miranda Card: Affirmed suppression — properly excluded
  • ✓ April 5 Statements: Affirmed suppression — no Miranda warnings given
  • ✓ Motion to Dismiss: Affirmed denial — dismissal not warranted
Legal Principle(s)

This is the binding precedent established by this case

  1. Exclusionary Rule Nexus Requirement: Evidence is excluded only when obtained "as a result" of official misconduct — there must be a causal relationship between the illegality and the evidence.
  2. Attenuation Doctrine: Where the nexus between police misconduct and discovery of challenged evidence is so attenuated as to dissipate the taint, such evidence is admissible.
  3. No Custody = No Miranda: Miranda warnings are not required when a defendant is not in police custody.
  4. Oral Waiver Sufficiency: A valid oral waiver of Miranda rights is sufficient even if the defendant does not sign a written waiver card.
  5. Indictment Dismissal Standard: Dismissal of an indictment requires egregious misconduct AND prejudice to the defendant's rights — not merely police misconduct.
  6. Remedial vs. Punitive Measures: Judicial responses to police misconduct should be limited to truly remedial (not punitive) measures tailored to cure prejudice to the defendant.
Court's Analysis

This is often the longest section - explain the court's logic step by step

1. Standard of Review — Subsidiary Findings

The SJC applied the "clear error" standard: subsidiary findings of fact by the motion judge are accepted unless clearly erroneous. The judge who saw and heard the witnesses is best positioned to assess credibility. No clear error in finding the signature was forged.

2. April 2 Statements — The Causation Problem

The court emphasized that the exclusionary rule requires a causal nexus between misconduct and evidence:

"The purpose of the exclusionary rule is to deter official misconduct by barring the use of evidence obtained as a result of the illegal action. The rule is predicated on a causal relationship between the acquisition of the evidence and the official transgression."

Here, the judge found:

  • Defendant was NOT in custody (so Miranda not required)
  • Defendant validly waived rights orally before the card was presented
  • The forged card did not assist police in obtaining statements
  • No prejudice to defendant's rights

Therefore, the April 2 statements were not the "fruit" of the forgery misconduct.

3. April 5 Statements — Miranda Violation

The Commonwealth conceded this issue: if the judge's factual finding that no Miranda warnings were given on April 5 stands, suppression is required. The SJC found no clear error in this credibility determination. Affirmed.

4. Motion to Dismiss — The Manning Standard

The court distinguished Commonwealth v. Manning (1977), which required dismissal only for:

  • Egregious misconduct AND
  • Prejudice to defendant's rights

In Manning, federal agents deliberately attacked the attorney-client relationship. Here:

  • The forgery did not impair the defendant's ability to obtain a fair trial
  • Suppression of the Miranda card purged the taint
  • Lesser remedies were sufficient
"Absent egregious misconduct or at least a serious threat of prejudice, the remedy of dismissal infringes too severely on the public interest in bringing guilty persons to justice."

Importance & Impact
  • Clarifies Exclusionary Rule Scope: Reinforces that not all police misconduct requires exclusion of evidence — causation is key.
  • Attenuation Doctrine Application: Shows Massachusetts courts require direct nexus between misconduct and evidence for suppression.
  • Miranda Waiver Rules: Establishes that oral waivers are valid even when written documentation is fraudulent or defective.
  • Dismissal Standard: Strengthens the high bar for dismissing indictments — prosecutors get multiple chances to cure misconduct through lesser remedies.
  • Police Accountability: Acknowledges seriousness of forgery but channels accountability through civil remedies and departmental discipline rather than dismissal.
  • Judicial Remedy Limitations: Emphasizes that judicial responses should be remedial, not punitive.
Additional Notes
  • Procedural Context: This was an interlocutory appeal under Mass. R. Crim. P. 15(b)(2) — both sides appealed different parts of the motion judge's ruling.
  • The Irony: The "Miranda card misadventure" (as the judge called it) was serious enough to note but not serious enough to warrant the defendant's preferred remedy of dismissal.
  • Handwriting Evidence: Two experts agreed the signature was forged, though the Commonwealth's expert couldn't say definitively due to a faulty pen.
  • Post-Hine Developments: Subsequent cases (Cinelli, Lam Hue To) continued to apply the Manning/Hine standard requiring both egregious misconduct AND prejudice for dismissal.
  • Judge's Dilemma: The motion judge explicitly stated he saw no prejudice but suppressed the statements anyway to "strike a balance" — the SJC rejected this punitive approach.
