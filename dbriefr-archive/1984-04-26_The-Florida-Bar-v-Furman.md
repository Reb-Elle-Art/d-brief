Title: The Florida Bar v. Furman
Citation: 451 So. 2d 808 (Fla. 1984)
Court: Supreme Court of Florida
Date: April 26, 1984
Rehearing: Denied July 11, 1984

Summary:
The Florida Supreme Court held Rosemary W. Furman in indirect criminal contempt for repeatedly engaging in the unauthorized practice of law (preparing pleadings, advising clients, instructing on filing and litigation strategy) in violation of a prior injunction. The Court affirmed the referee's findings of contempt but reduced the sentence recommended by the referee to a 30-day county jail term (with 90 days suspended) and taxed costs against Furman.

FACTS:
Respondent Furman operated a profit-making clerical/"do-it-yourself divorce kit" business and was not licensed to practice law. After a 1979 decision enjoining certain activities, the Florida Bar charged Furman with further instances of unauthorized practice in 1982 and 1983. Testimony from former customers—largely uncontradicted—showed Furman advised clients to falsify information, to conceal facts from courts, prepared pleadings that raised substantive legal issues (custody, support, property division), instructed where and how to file, and corrected defective pleadings for a fee (up to $100). In one instance she advised a husband not to withdraw a stipulation despite known child abuse, advising him to let social services handle it.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY:
- 1977: Initial Bar petitions alleging unauthorized practice; referee and then this Court found Furman engaged in unauthorized practice; injunction issued (reported at 376 So. 2d 378 (Fla. 1979)).
- Sept. 28, 1982 & Mar. 9, 1983: Bar filed additional petitions alleging multiple violations of the injunction; rules to show cause issued; referee appointed; evidentiary hearing held June 20-21, 1983.
- Referee found multiple violations and recommended criminal contempt with concurrent four-month prison sentences.
- This Court reviewed the referee's report and imposed a reduced sentence: 30 days in Duval County jail, with 90 days suspended contingent on two years of compliance.

ISSUES:
1. Did respondent have a Sixth Amendment right to a jury trial for alleged indirect criminal contempt punishable under the Bar's Integration Rule (potentially up to five months' imprisonment and fines)?
2. Were the referee's findings supported by evidence and did they apply a proper legal standard in determining violation of the earlier injunction (Furman/Brumbaugh)?
3. Were defenses of laches or procedural irregularity sufficient to dismiss any counts?
4. Was the referee's sentencing process (appearing to have decided sentence before mitigation evidence) improper?

HOLDING:
The Court held Furman in indirect criminal contempt for violating the injunction against unauthorized practice of law. It rejected claims to a jury trial and to laches or procedural dismissal. The Court imposed a single 30-day county jail term (with 90 days suspended) rather than the referee's recommended concurrent four-month prison terms; costs taxed to respondent.

RULE OF LAW:
- A valid injunction must be obeyed until vacated or modified; violation may be punished by contempt. (Seaboard Air Line R.R. v. Tampa Southern R.R., cited.)
- When the judge acts as trier of fact in contempt proceedings, the offense is reduced to a petty offense and a jury trial is not required so long as the actual sentence does not exceed six months (Aaron v. State; Taylor v. Hayes).
- The scope of the earlier injunction (as derived from Brumbaugh) forbids advising clients on remedies, preparing or correcting forms that affect substantive rights, and instructing on filing or presenting evidence; selling forms and typing completed instruments was permissible, but personal legal assistance in conjunction with business activities was not.

REASONING / ANALYSIS:
- Jury trial: The Court relied on Aaron and Taylor to conclude that the right to jury trial is controlled by the actual penalty imposed, not merely the potential maximum; because respondent had been denied jury trial earlier and the recommended sentence fell below the constitutional ceiling for judge-tried contempt (and the Court could reduce punishment), no Sixth Amendment violation arose. The Court rejected the argument that potential fines alone trigger jury rights (citing Muniz v. Hoffman).
- Evidence/support for contempt: The referee's detailed, case-by-case findings—based largely on uncontradicted customer testimony and documentary forms—supported the determination that Furman repeatedly engaged in activities exceeding mere clerical assistance and continued to violate the injunction. The Court adopted the referee's findings.
- Laches/procedure: The Court found laches not proven; investigatory and filing procedures leading to the second petition were adequate under article XVI and did not deprive respondent of due process.
- Sentencing: The Court rejected the referee's harsher sentence as excessive under the circumstances and exercised its discretion to impose a lesser county jail term with portion suspended on condition of compliance for two years.

DISPOSITION:
Respondent Rosemary W. Furman is adjudged in indirect criminal contempt for violating the Court's prior injunction against unauthorized practice of law. She is sentenced to 30 days in Duval County jail; 90 days suspended if she complies with the injunction for two years. Costs taxed to Furman.

SIGNIFICANCE / NOTES:
- The opinion reaffirms that nonlawyer legal assistance crossing into advising, interpreting statutes, preparing or correcting pleadings that affect substantive rights, or instructing on litigation strategy constitutes unauthorized practice and may support contempt sanctions.
- The Court emphasizes obedience to injunctions and the limited role of laches or procedural minutiae where the merits of disobedience are established.
- The case illustrates the Court's willingness to calibrate contempt sentences to constitutional limits while affirming findings of misconduct.
